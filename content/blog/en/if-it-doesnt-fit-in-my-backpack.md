My whole wardrobe now fits in :hand-drawn{svg="/blog/doodles/blog_medium_underline.svg" text-layer="under"}[one backpack]. And it was the best decision I never planned.

Look, I had been thinking about this for a long time: I hate :hand-drawn{svg="/blog/doodles/blog_medium_circle.svg" placement="around" text-layer="under"}[fast fashion], having way too many things, and compulsive buying. On a human level and an environmental one. But at the same time, I have always liked expressing myself through clothes and dressing well. And dressing well does not mean buying the most expensive stuff or following trends. It means having personality in how you dress, some coherence, your own style, making clothes your own...

And that led me to hunt for weird pieces, things I kept finding, and in the end, to accumulate. Because a lot of what got damaged I would customize and wear even more, but I was also finding new things and giving myself little treats. And that ended with me owning a ton of clothes. Way more than I need, or than anyone needs.

## The move that forced me to decide

That feeling of owning more than I need and still buying more always gave me guilt, but I never fully made the call. Until a few weeks ago, when I had to move. Damn, that is when the feeling blew up.

Seeing everything I had to carry from one place to another, seeing everything thrown around, not knowing where to put it, the stress of spending days with everything all over the house... made me realize I could not keep going like that, going against my values because my mind is chaotic and because I like everything. And even less could I accept that something material was causing me that level of stress and discomfort. That is a hard no.

I decided to keep the basics, donate the rest, and store a few seasonal items in storage. Not much, no more than two pieces. Because a heavy winter jacket has no business in summer, and when winter comes, a denim vest has no business then either. For donating, since I know it is hard for me to let go of clothes because of that slightly dumb emotional attachment humans have to things, I set a three-month rule: if in three months it does not cross my mind to go get it from storage, I donate it without hesitation. And once I started choosing, I realized I actually already knew what should stay.

## What stays in my backpack

10 basic T-shirts: black, white, and yellow. All plain or with very little detail. And it is not because of minimalism, or because I do not like cool graphic tees. It is because I am pretty tattooed, and mixing a printed T-shirt with tattooed skin often ends up looking like visual noise to me. Tattoos are already an important part of the outfit without taking up space, and clothes end up being the background.

10 boxers. Here I like loose boxers, with playful patterns and weird prints. 10 pairs of socks: mostly white sports socks, with a few loud and special ones. They are tiny details people do not usually see, but when they do (which always ends up happening haha), I like them to stand out and look good. That is my basic setup to do laundry once a week. I would keep less, but since I train, depending on the day and time, I might need two changes.

3 sweatpants, 3 dressier pants, 5 hoodies. All with hoods: 2 zip-ups and 3 pullovers. Every piece chosen with care, every piece with its own personality, its own story, and its own reason to be there.

For shoes, I kept three pairs: gym sneakers, which are also my day-to-day shoes; white and yellow Nikes that I have a kind of affection for I cannot fully explain, but that fit perfectly with my base colors (black, yellow, and white), match absolutely everything, and that is not random; and classic Dr. Martens for when something more serious is needed.

With jackets, the rule was the same: one black bomber, one customized leather jacket, one black vest and one black denim jacket with patches, one light denim jacket with tears stitched with yellow fabric underneath (because a tear does not have to be the end of a garment), and one long black coat for real cold days. Cold or warm, no in-between, no "just in case" jackets. If I am cold, I wear a hoodie under it; if I am warm, I take it off.

## Buy less, care more

I try not to buy these pieces just anywhere. I like supporting :hand-drawn{svg="/blog/doodles/blog_medium_circle.svg" placement="around" width="103%" stroke-width="4.5" text-layer="under"}[local businesses] and finding more unique items. And, why not say it, often better ones too. But like everyone else, I still have some stuff from the big stores we all know, and little by little I hope to phase those out.

And little by little, the pieces that are not already customized, I will keep customizing. Or patching up, so they last longer and I do not replace them at the first chance. A stain can become a new patch, a tear can become a new seam.

## The new standard

Look, the backpack is still big, I noticed while reviewing this haha, but I hope over time I will need an even smaller one. Right now I am really happy with the decision and with the new standard: :hand-drawn{svg="/blog/doodles/blog_long_underline.svg" stroke-width="4" text-layer="under"}[if it does not fit in my backpack, I do not need it]. And if I want something new, I know I need to remove something else and donate it, not hide it where I cannot see it haha.

Because accumulating is the easy path. It is what everyone does by default, without thinking. Choosing what defines you and letting go of the rest asks for a bit more honesty with yourself than we :hand-drawn{svg="/blog/doodles/blog_asterisk.svg" placement="right" trigger="scroll" width="1.4em" text-layer="under"}[usually want to admit.]

In the end, it is like when you are working on a project and realize you have too much noise. Sometimes you have to delete code that already exists, cut what adds no value even if it cost effort to build. Clothes are the same. The difference is that here, instead of ending up in a trash bin, it ends up in the hands of someone who actually needs it.
